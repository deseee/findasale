# Photo Capture Protocol — FindA.Sale Organizer Flow
**Session 350 | UX Copy & Photo Guidance System**

---

## Overview

This protocol replaces the single-line "it's too dark" error with a comprehensive, guided photo-taking experience. It addresses the reality that most FindA.Sale organizers are 50–70 years old, non-photographers, shooting in cluttered rooms with variable lighting on a phone.

Every piece of in-app copy, every shot sequence, and every lighting guidance is designed to feel like a knowledgeable friend helping them take better photos—not a tech system criticizing their photos.

**Core principle:** The organizer's job is to photograph. The app's job is to guide them so the AI can work, and buyers get the information they need.

---

## SECTION 1: THE SHOT SEQUENCE

The shot sequence is presented as a **numbered workflow** on the camera screen. Not all shots are required for every item, but this order maximizes AI accuracy and buyer confidence. Each shot is **Required**, **Recommended**, or **Optional**.

---

### Shot 1: Hero / Front Shot
**Required**

- **What to capture:** The main face of the item—the side a buyer would see first in a listing photo. This is your listing thumbnail.
- **Why it matters:** Buyers skim listings in 2 seconds. A clear front view is the make-or-break first impression. The AI uses this to identify the item type, condition, and color.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Frame the entire item in the center of the screen (don't cut off edges).
  - Step back far enough so the item fills 60–70% of the frame (not tiny, not cropped).
  - Keep it level (don't tilt sideways).
  - If it's a 3D object, face the most interesting or colorful side toward the camera.
- **In-app guidance copy (shown before capture):**
  > "Get the full front view. Step back so the whole item fits in frame, centered."

---

### Shot 2: Back Shot
**Recommended** (unless item is flat: art, documents, textiles)

- **What to capture:** The reverse or back side of the item.
- **Why it matters:** Buyers want to know what the back looks like (finish quality, maker information, wear patterns). The AI detects maker's marks and condition issues on the back.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Turn the item around (or rotate your camera position).
  - Use the same framing as the front shot (full item, centered, level).
  - If the back looks identical to the front, take a quick shot anyway so the AI can confirm both sides match.
- **In-app guidance copy (shown after shot 1, before shot 2):**
  > "Now the back. This helps buyers see the full condition and any maker's marks."

---

### Shot 3: Left Side
**Recommended** (for 3D items only: furniture, ceramics, electronics)

- **What to capture:** The left side profile of the item, showing depth, shape, and any left-side details.
- **Why it matters:** For 3D objects, a side view confirms the item's real shape and scale. The AI uses this to estimate dimensions and spot damage on edges.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Rotate the item 90 degrees left (or move your camera to shoot the left edge).
  - Keep the item straight (not angled).
  - Include the top and bottom edge to show height and depth.
- **In-app guidance copy:**
  > "Rotate left. This shows the item's depth and shape from the side."

---

### Shot 4: Right Side
**Recommended** (for 3D items only: furniture, ceramics, electronics)

- **What to capture:** The right side profile of the item.
- **Why it matters:** Confirms symmetry (or lack thereof) and reveals damage/wear on the right side that the left didn't show. Buyers want to know if an item has a "best" and "worst" side.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Rotate to the right side (opposite of shot 3).
  - Match the angle and height of shot 3 so shots 3 and 4 are mirror frames.
  - Include the full height and depth.
- **In-app guidance copy:**
  > "Rotate right. Show the other side profile."

---

### Shot 5: Maker's Mark / Label / Signature
**Recommended** (highly boosts AI tagging and buyer trust)

- **What to capture:** Any maker's name, signature, hallmark, date, brand logo, or identifying stamp—closeup.
- **Why it matters:** This is gold. A maker's mark tells the buyer (and the AI) exactly what it is and often its age/value. Brands matter for jewelry, furniture, art, ceramics, appliances.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Move very close to the mark (6 inches from camera).
  - Use your phone's auto-focus (tap the mark on screen to focus).
  - Make sure the text/stamp is crisp and readable.
  - If the mark is small, zoom in digitally (pinch) on your phone **before** tapping capture—but no more than 2x zoom.
  - Shoot it straight-on, not at an angle.
- **In-app guidance copy:**
  > "Look for a maker's name, logo, or stamp. Close-up photo of the mark. This is the most valuable shot—it helps us identify the brand."

---

### Shot 6: Damage Closeup
**Recommended** (honesty builds trust)

- **What to capture:** Any chips, cracks, stains, tears, dents, scratches, fading, or wear—closeup.
- **Why it matters:** Buyers trust sellers who are honest about flaws. Hiding damage in photos leads to returns and negative reviews. The AI flags damage to help with condition grading. Transparency = higher prices long-term.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Zoom in on the problem area (6–8 inches from the damage).
  - Use good lighting (move near a window if the damage is hard to see).
  - Get the damage and some surrounding area in frame so the buyer can see where the damage is on the item.
  - If there are multiple damage spots, you can take multiple closeups or pick the most visible one.
  - Don't hide it—show it clearly.
- **In-app guidance copy:**
  > "Any chips, cracks, stains, or wear? Take a close photo. Honest damage photos build buyer trust."

---

### Shot 7: Detail / Pattern Closeup
**Recommended** (decorative items, jewelry, textiles, art)

- **What to capture:** A beautiful detail—inlay, carving, pattern, engraving, stitching, weave, brushwork, or any decorative element that shows quality or craftsmanship.
- **Why it matters:** Details sell. A closeup of intricate carving or beautiful embroidery tells buyers this is a quality piece. The AI uses this to boost condition and rarity estimates.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Find the most interesting or beautiful part of the item.
  - Move close (4–6 inches) and focus on texture, pattern, or fine details.
  - Use even lighting—if the detail is in shadow, move near a window or lamp.
  - Avoid shadows from your hand or phone blocking the light.
- **In-app guidance copy:**
  > "Any beautiful details? Carving, embroidery, pattern, or design? A closeup shows craftsmanship and quality."

---

### Shot 8: Scale Reference
**Optional** (very helpful for furniture, glassware, collectibles)

- **What to capture:** The item next to your hand, a coin, a ruler, or common object (phone, pen, credit card) that shows size.
- **Why it matters:** Buyers can't judge size from a photo alone. A vase next to a hand tells them "that's 6 inches tall." Furniture next to a person tells them the height. Scale drives buying decisions.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Place a recognizable object next to the item (your hand, a quarter, a standard playing card).
  - Shoot from the side so both item and reference object are clearly visible.
  - Make sure the reference object is completely in frame.
  - Alternative: stand in the frame next to the item for large furniture (one full photo of you + furniture).
- **In-app guidance copy:**
  > "Help buyers judge size. Place your hand next to it, or a coin. Or stand in the photo if it's large."

---

### Shot 9: Inside / Underside
**Optional** (especially important for furniture, vessels, jewelry boxes, collectibles)

- **What to capture:** The interior of a drawer, the underside of a table, the bottom of a vase, the inside of a jewelry box, or the underside of jewelry.
- **Why it matters:** Drawers tell you if a piece is solid wood or veneer. Undersides show construction method, wood type, and wear. Interiors reveal upholstery condition, odors, or repair history.
- **How to get it right:**
  - Open drawers or flip/turn the item to show the interior or underside.
  - Use good lighting (natural window light is best; avoid shadows from your hand).
  - Keep the phone level and frame the interior clearly.
  - For small items (jewelry, watches), use the phone's zoom to get a closeup of the inside mechanism or hallmark.
- **In-app guidance copy:**
  > "Open any drawers? Flip it to show the underside? Interiors tell buyers about quality and condition."

---

## SECTION 2: BACKGROUND GUIDANCE

Background directly affects AI tag accuracy and photo quality. Provide context-aware guidance based on what the organizer is seeing.

---

### Ideal Background

- **What it looks like:**
  - Plain or softly blurred background (neutral white, soft gray, natural shadow, or fabric).
  - No clutter, competing objects, or distracting colors behind the item.
  - The item is the clear focal point.
  - Light is even and doesn't create harsh shadows on the background.

- **In-app tip copy:**
  > "Perfect lighting and background. You're all set—ready to capture."

---

### Cluttered Background (Most Common Estate Sale Scenario)

- **Why it harms AI accuracy:**
  - The AI struggles to isolate the item from surrounding clutter.
  - Tags become vague ("furniture" instead of "oak dresser").
  - Buyer photos look amateur and less trustworthy.
  - Multiple items in the frame confuse the AI—it doesn't know what you're trying to sell.

- **Quick fix (1–2 steps):**
  1. **Move the item.** Carry it to a clearer spot (even a blank wall, a white sheet, or a window sill).
  2. **If you can't move it:** Place a white pillowcase, white poster board, or light-colored fabric *behind* the item to block clutter.
  3. **Reframe.** Step back and angle your camera so the background is mostly blank.

- **In-app tip copy:**
  > "Busy background behind the item. Quick fix: step back, reframe, or move it to a clear spot. This helps our AI identify it better."

---

### Reflective / Shiny Items (Mirrors, Silver, Glass, Chrome)

- **Why it's hard to photograph:**
  - The camera's light reflects back, creating blown-out (overexposed) spots.
  - The AI sees bright spots instead of the item's actual surface.
  - Silver and mirrors lose detail in overexposed photos.

- **How to reduce glare:**
  1. **Move away from direct light.** Avoid harsh sunlight or bright lamps shining on the shiny surface.
  2. **Use soft, diffused light.** Shoot near a window but not in direct sun, or use an indoor lamp at an angle (not head-on).
  3. **Angle the camera slightly.** Shoot from a 30–45-degree angle rather than straight-on. This changes how light reflects.
  4. **Use a matte background.** Dark or neutral cloth behind it reduces reflections.

- **In-app tip copy:**
  > "Shiny item? Avoid harsh direct light. Shoot near a window for soft light, or angle your camera 45 degrees to reduce glare."

---

### Dark / Heavy Wood Furniture

- **Why auto-exposure fails:**
  - Your phone's camera assumes the whole frame is evenly lit. Dark wood tricks it into over-brightening and washing out the photo.
  - The item looks flat and colorless instead of rich and detailed.

- **How to fix:**
  1. **Move near a window.** Natural window light is the best for wood—it shows grain and color.
  2. **Tap to expose.** Before capturing, tap on the darkest part of the wood on your screen. This tells the phone "expose for this dark area" instead of the whole frame.
  3. **Avoid spotlights.** A single bright lamp creates hard shadows. Better: soft light from a large window.
  4. **Use a lighter background.** Light-colored cloth or wall behind it makes the dark wood stand out more.

- **In-app tip copy:**
  > "Dark wood shows best in natural window light. Before capture, tap on the wood to set exposure. Avoid harsh spotlights."

---

### Textiles / Clothing on Floor vs. Hanging

- **Which is better for AI tagging:**
  - **Hanging:** Better. Hanging a garment, quilt, or rug lets the AI see the full shape, how it drapes, and details without wrinkles or folds hiding the pattern.
  - **On floor:** Usable but suboptimal. Folds and wrinkles distort the pattern and shape. The AI may misidentify size or condition.

- **In-app tip copy:**
  > "Textiles photograph better hanging. If you hang it, the AI can see the full pattern and shape clearly. Flat on the floor works too, but smooth it out first."

---

### Outdoor Shots (Yard Sale / Flea Market)

- **Best time of day:**
  - **Golden hour** (early morning or 1–2 hours before sunset): Soft, warm light that shows details without harsh shadows.
  - **Avoid:** Noon (harsh shadows, blown-out highlights, squinting in bright sun).
  - **Cloudy days:** Perfect. Overcast acts as a giant diffuser—soft, even light everywhere.

- **Avoiding harsh shadows:**
  - Position the item so the sun is at a **45-degree angle** to the camera, not directly behind or ahead of the item.
  - Avoid shooting when the sun casts a sharp shadow of your phone or body on the item.
  - If shooting at noon and you must, position yourself or an assistant to cast a soft shadow over the item (reduces glare and harsh shadows).

- **In-app tip copy:**
  > "Outdoor sales? Morning or late afternoon light is best. Avoid harsh noon sun. Move the item out of direct shadows."

## SECTION 3: LIGHTING GUIDANCE (REPLACE "IT'S TOO DARK")

The current system flags dark photos with a single-line error. Replace it with a **tiered, educational system** that tells organizers exactly what's wrong and what to do.

### Tier 1 — Good Lighting (No Warning)

**Trigger:** Average image brightness ≥ 65 (on 0–100 scale) **and** no severe motion blur **and** no extreme glare.

**In-app confirmation (shown after capture):**
> "Perfect lighting. You've got great detail here—our system can work with this."

**Action:** Auto-proceed. No friction. Positive reinforcement.

---

### Tier 2 — Soft Warning (Photo Usable, Not Ideal)

**Trigger:** Average brightness 40–65 **OR** slight motion blur **OR** mild glare/hotspots.

**In-app copy (shown after capture):**
> "The light's a bit soft. We can still work with this, but a brighter spot might give you better results. Want to try again near a window or lamp?"

**Action:**
- Allow upload (not a blocker).
- Offer two buttons: **"Use This Photo"** (green, friendly) and **"Retake in Better Light"** (secondary).
- Default selection: "Use This Photo" (organizer is in control, not being shamed).

---

### Tier 3 — Hard Warning (Photo Likely to Fail AI)

**Trigger:** Average brightness < 40 **OR** severe motion blur **OR** extreme glare/multiple blown-out areas.

**In-app copy (shown after capture):**
> "This photo is too dark for our AI to identify the item properly. Here's the fix: Move to a well-lit area (near a window or under a lamp) and try again. It only takes 10 seconds."

**Action:**
- Block upload (don't let them push a photo that will fail).
- Show the **exact problem:** *"Too dark"* or *"Motion blur"* or *"Too much glare"*.
- Offer two buttons: **"Retake"** (primary, prominent) and **"Skip This Item"** (small secondary—for rare cases).
- Auto-launch the camera app again so they don't have to re-navigate.

---

### In-App Lighting Hints (Pre-Capture, on Viewfinder)

Before the organizer taps the shutter, show a real-time indicator of lighting quality. This educates them proactively.

**Display a small indicator at the top of the camera viewfinder:**

```
[●●●●●] Good lighting—ready to capture
[●●●○○] Soft lighting—might work, but brighter is better
[●○○○○] Too dark—move near a window or lamp
```

**Copy next to indicator:**
> "Lighting indicator: Move closer to a window or lamp if it shows red. Green is ready."

This removes guesswork and teaches them as they frame the shot.

## SECTION 4: AI FEEDBACK AFTER ANALYSIS

After the organizer uploads a photo, the backend analyzes it with the Vision API (Claude's vision model) and suggests tags, category, condition, and description. Present each result contextually.

---

### High Confidence Result (AI Confident in Tags)

**Trigger:** AI confidence score ≥ 80% on primary classification.

**In-app copy (shown on review screen):**
> "We identified this as a [item type]. Here's what we found: [tags]. Does this match? You can edit any of these below."

**Action:**
- Display the AI-generated tags prominently (bold, clear).
- Show a confidence meter as a simple visual: **[████████ 85% confident]**.
- Pre-fill the form fields (title, category, condition, description) but make them all editable.
- Emphasize the organizer's control: "Does this match? You can edit any of these below."

**Example copy:**
> "We identified this as a **wooden dresser**. Victorian style, oak finish, 4-drawer. Good condition, minor scratches on top surface. Does this match? Edit anything that doesn't."

---

### Medium Confidence Result (AI Unsure)

**Trigger:** AI confidence 50–79% on primary classification.

**In-app copy:**
> "We think this is a [item type], but we're not 100% sure. Here's our best guess. Please review and confirm or correct the details below."

**Action:**
- Display tags but mark them as **suggestions**, not certainties.
- Highlight fields that need verification: **"Condition?"** (with a help icon).
- Use softer language: *"We think"* instead of *"This is"*.
- Encourage the organizer to correct: "Seen something like this before? Help us get it right."

**Example copy:**
> "We think this might be a **porcelain vase**, but the angle and lighting make it hard to be sure. Is that right? Please confirm the type, color, and condition below. You know this item better than we do."

---

### Low Confidence Result (AI Couldn't Identify Item)

**Trigger:** AI confidence < 50% or unable to classify.

**In-app copy:**
> "We couldn't quite identify this one from the photo. No problem—just tell us what it is, and we'll fill in what we can. A clearer photo (or a different angle) might help too."

**Action:**
- Don't show confusing AI guesses (it frustrates organizers).
- Ask the organizer to describe it: *"What is this? E.g., 'wooden chair', 'ceramic vase', 'vintage radio'."*
- Offer to re-analyze if they retake the photo: **"Retake with better lighting?"** (secondary button).
- Don't make them feel like they failed—frame it as a learning moment for the AI: "We learn from you—your input makes our system smarter."

**Example copy:**
> "We couldn't identify this from the photo. No worries—just tell us what it is (e.g., 'oak dresser' or 'Chinese porcelain vase'). A closer or clearer photo would help too, but no pressure."

---

### Unexpected Item Type Detected

**Trigger:** AI is very confident in a classification, but it's unusual or wrong (e.g., AI thinks it's a "lamp" but organizer photographed a "vase").

**In-app copy:**
> "We think this is a [unexpected item], but it looks like you might have meant something else. What did you photograph? We can re-analyze."

**Action:**
- Show the AI's guess (non-judgmental).
- Offer a corrective flow: **"Yes, that's right"** or **"No, it's actually [organizer types]"**.
- If they correct it, re-run the AI analysis on the same photo with the correct item type.
- Normalize mistakes: "No problem—we'll tag it correctly."

**Example copy:**
> "We detected a lamp here. Is that right? Or did you mean to tag a different item? Just let us know, and we'll adjust."

---

### Maker's Mark / Label Detected

**Trigger:** OCR or vision model detects readable text (brand, signature, hallmark, date).

**In-app copy:**
> "We found a maker's mark: [detected text]. This is a big deal—it tells buyers exactly what they're getting and helps with value. Make sure we got it right below."

**Action:**
- Display the detected text prominently (bold, highlighted).
- Verify it with the organizer (they can see it in the photo and confirm or correct).
- Auto-fill brand/maker field based on the text.
- Celebrate this: "Finding maker's marks is one of the best ways to sell vintage items at the right price."

**Example copy:**
> "Found it: **Wedgwood** (maker's mark on the bottom). This is valuable info—Wedgwood pottery is highly collectible. We've filled in the brand. Confirm it's correct?"

---

### Damage Detected in Photo

**Trigger:** Vision model detects obvious damage (cracks, chips, stains, dents, wear).

**In-app copy:**
> "We spotted some wear: [damage description]. We marked it in the condition field. Honest damage photos build trust—keep this visible in your listing, and buyers will trust your pricing."

**Action:**
- Auto-fill the **Condition** field (e.g., "Good—minor chips on rim").
- Highlight the damage in the photo (visual annotation if possible).
- Reinforce honesty: "Transparency = trust = higher prices long-term. Hiding damage leads to returns."
- Allow editing: organizer can downgrade condition if damage is worse, or upgrade if it's minor.

**Example copy:**
> "We see a chip on the rim and light crazing. We've marked this as 'Good condition with light damage.' Keep this visible in your photos—honest sellers build loyal buyers who trust their pricing."

## SECTION 5: MULTI-PHOTO SESSION GUIDANCE

Most items benefit from 3–5 photos. Guide the organizer through the session without being pushy.

---

### First Photo Just Taken (Hero Shot)

**In-app copy:**
> "Great first shot! This will be your listing photo. Want to add a back view, maker's mark, or damage closeup to build buyer confidence?"

**Display small thumbnail strip below the camera view:**
- Slot 1: Hero shot (✓ captured)
- Slot 2: Back (suggest next)
- Slots 3–5: Optional (sides, mark, detail)

**Suggested action:** Auto-switch to camera mode for shot 2 (don't close the camera).

---

### After 2 Photos

**In-app copy:**
> "Two down! Want to add a photo of any maker's mark or label? Or any damage to be honest about? Either will make your listing stronger."

**Display progress indicator:**
> "Photos: 2 of 3 (minimum) / 5 (ideal)"

**Action:** Suggest the **highest-value next shot:**
- If no mark found yet: "Look for a maker's mark—this is gold."
- If no damage shown: "Any chips, cracks, or wear? Show them—honest photos = trusted sellers."
- Otherwise: "Want to add a side view or detail photo?"

---

### After 3 Photos (Minimum Met)

**In-app copy:**
> "You're good! You've got the minimum. Want to add one more detail shot, or are you ready to review and tag?"

**Display motivation:**
> "👏 You've captured the essentials. More photos = higher prices. Your choice."

**Buttons:** **"Add One More"** (secondary) | **"Ready to Review"** (primary, green).

**Default action:** Proceed to review screen if they wait 3 seconds without tapping (auto-proceed, no nagging).

---

### After 5+ Photos

**In-app copy:**
> "You've done a thorough job. Five or more photos tell the complete story of this item. Ready to review and tag?"

**Celebrate:**
> "⭐ You're a pro—five photos is excellent. Ready to review?"

**Action:** Show a prominent **"Review & Tag"** button. Disable "Add Another" (they've done enough; more is diminishing returns).

---

### "You're Done" Moment (Proceeding to Review)

When the organizer taps "Ready to Review," summarize what they've captured and preview the next step.

**In-app copy:**
> "Perfect! You've captured [N] photos of [item]. Let's review them and add details like price and condition. Any of these photos feel wrong? You can retake before we analyze."

**Display a small carousel of thumbnails** (all the photos they took, in order). Allow them to:
- **Retake** any shot (tap the X on the thumbnail).
- **Proceed** to AI analysis (tap "Next").

**Reassurance:**
> "We'll analyze all [N] photos to give you the best tags and description. You can edit everything—we're just getting you started."

**Clothing / Accessories**
> "Clothing quality shows in fabric and details. Full front and back (hanging is best), fabric content tag, any damage or stains, and a closeup of the seams or stitching."

## SECTION 7: IMPLEMENTATION NOTES FOR DEVELOPERS

### Toast / Warning System

Replace the current single-line "It's too dark" toast with a **tiered toast system**:

```
Tier 1 (brightness >= 65):
  → No toast. Auto-proceed.

Tier 2 (40 <= brightness < 65):
  → Toast: "The light's a bit soft. Brighter spot might help. Want to try again?"
  → Buttons: "Use This" | "Retake"
  → Default: "Use This" (selected)
  → Timeout: No auto-dismiss. Organizer controls.

Tier 3 (brightness < 40):
  → Modal (not toast—deserves attention).
  → Title: "Too dark to identify"
  → Copy: "Move to a well-lit area and try again. It takes 10 seconds."
  → Buttons: "Retake" | "Skip This Item"
  → Default: "Retake"
  → Behavior: Auto-launch camera on "Retake"
```

### UI Indicators

**Pre-capture viewfinder indicator (top of camera screen):**
```
Real-time brightness sampling:
  - Sample 64x64 pixel patch from center of frame
  - Calculate average brightness (0–100)
  - Display indicator:
    Green [●●●●●]:   >= 65 (good)
    Yellow [●●●○○]:  40–65 (soft)
    Red [●○○○○]:     < 40 (too dark)
```

**Post-capture review carousel:**
- Show thumbnail strip of all captured photos.
- Mark "Retake" option on each.
- Show small badge on each: "✓ Good" (Tier 1), "⚠️ Soft" (Tier 2), "❌ Too Dark" (Tier 3).

```
   ↓
5. If Tier 1 or Tier 2 "Use This": upload to /api/items/upload-photo
   ↓
6. API returns photoUrl, triggers vision analysis
   ↓
7. PreviewModal displays AI results (high/medium/low confidence)
   ↓
8. Organizer reviews AI-generated fields, edits as needed
   ↓
9. Tap "Save Item" → POST /api/items/{id} with edits
```

### Copy Tone Guidelines

- **No jargon.** Never say "underexposed," "insufficient luminance," "confidence score," or "OCR."
- **Friendly, not technical.** "The light's a bit soft" not "Average brightness = 52."
- **Empower the organizer.** "You know this item better than we do" not "Please correct our AI."
- **Celebrate wins.** "Perfect lighting" / "Great first shot" / "Five photos is excellent."
- **Normalize mistakes.** "No problem" / "We learn from you" / "This one's tricky—let's try a different angle."
- **Action-focused.** Every warning tells them what to do: "Move near a window" / "Take a closeup" / "Flip it to show the inside."

## APPENDIX: Brightness Threshold Reference

| Brightness Score | Visual Appearance | Tier | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0–20 | Pitch black, unusable | (blocked) | Don't allow capture |
| 20–40 | Very dark, no details visible | Tier 3 | Hard warning modal. Strongly suggest retake. |
| 40–65 | Dim, details visible but dull | Tier 2 | Soft warning. Allow upload but suggest better light. |
| 65–80 | Good, clear details | Tier 1 | Good. Proceed. |
| 80–95 | Bright, maybe slightly overexposed | Tier 1 | Good. Proceed. |
| 95–100 | Blown out, blown highlights | Tier 2.5 | Warn about glare/overexposure. Allow but suggest angle/lighting fix. |
