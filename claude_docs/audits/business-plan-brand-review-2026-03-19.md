# FindA.Sale Business Plan — Brand Voice & Messaging Audit
**Date:** 2026-03-19
**Auditor:** Marketing Brand Voice Reviewer
**Status:** Complete Review with Actionable Feedback
**Scope:** BUSINESS_PLAN.md alignment with brand-voice-guide-2026-03-16.md

---

## Executive Summary

**Overall Assessment:** The business plan is **strategic and well-structured**, but **brand voice is inconsistent and sometimes contradicts the documented guide**. The document reads as a traditional financial plan with occasional warm language, rather than as a cohesive brand narrative that celebrates FindA.Sale's unified approach to ALL sale types.

**Key Findings:**
1. **Estate Sales Dominance:** Language skews heavily toward estate sales, treating other sale types (garage sales, auctions, flea markets) as afterthoughts or secondary mentions
2. **Missing Joy & Community:** The tone is professional but lacks the "treasure hunt" excitement and community celebration central to the brand
3. **Jargon Creep:** Uses corporate language ("marketplace," "monetization," "TAM/SAM/SOM") that violates the "no jargon" guideline
4. **Inconsistent Messaging Pillars:** The plan's core pillars don't align with the documented brand pillars (Reach, Simplicity, Fair Pricing for organizers; Discovery, Community, Value for shoppers)
5. **Competitive Narrative Misalignment:** Positioning emphasizes features and fees over the narrative of workflow stickiness that drives moat defensibility

**Recommendation:** Revise key sections (Executive Summary, Products overview, Market positioning, competitive advantages) to weave in brand voice naturally, celebrate all sale types equally, and emphasize community and discovery language over financial metrics.

## Detailed Brand Voice Assessment

### 1. TONE & VOICE CONSISTENCY

**Standard:** Professional yet approachable; warm, inclusive, practical. Conversational where possible. No corporate jargon.

**What the Business Plan Does Well:**
- Section 1 (Executive Summary) opening is warm: "FindA.Sale emerged from founder Patrick DeSee's direct observation of inefficiencies..." — personal, grounded
- Section 3 (Products overview) correctly names all sale types: "estate sales, yard sales, auctions, and flea markets" early and often
- Section 6 (Marketing) uses concrete, specific examples that feel human: "Margaret, Professional Estate Organizer" personas are relatable
- Section 7 (Operations) processes are clear and jargon-light

**What Needs Fixing:**
1. **Overuse of Business Jargon:**
   - "two-sided marketplace" (appears 3x) → brand guide says avoid corporate speak
   - "monetization" (Section 3) → use "how we earn" or "how the platform works financially"
   - "TAM/SAM/SOM" (Section 4) → acceptable in financial section, but could be introduced more conversationally
   - "LTV:CAC ratio" (Section 9) → financial jargon, fine for finance section but not in customer-facing sections
   - "blended fee rate" (Section 9) → simplify to "average platform fee"

   **Fix:** In sections 1–3 (customer-facing narrative), replace jargon with conversational equivalents.

2. **Tone Shifts Abruptly:**
   - Section 1 (warm, founder story): "FindA.Sale emerged from founder Patrick DeSee's direct observation..."
   - Section 2 (suddenly transactional): "Registered Address: Grand Rapids, Michigan (USA); Legal Structure: Michigan Limited Liability Company..."
   - Section 3 returns to warmth: "FindA.Sale is a unified estate sale marketplace..."

   **Fix:** Break sections into "strategic narrative" (warm, brand-voiced) and "operational details" (factual, structured). Create a clear visual hierarchy.

3. **Missing Conversational Voice:**
   - Plan uses passive voice: "The platform offers," "FindA.Sale is," "Shoppers get"
   - Brand guide calls for second-person, active voice: "You upload photos. AI tags them. You publish. You get paid."
   - Current: "FindA.Sale is a mobile-first Progressive Web App (PWA) connecting sale organizers (sellers) with shoppers (buyers)"
   - Better: "Upload photos. AI tags them. Publish once. Reach buyers. Get paid in hours, not weeks."

   **Fix:** Rewrite key product paragraphs in active voice, second-person where possible.

### 4. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING & MOAT NARRATIVE

**Brand Guideline:** Emphasize **workflow stickiness** and **unified platform advantage**, not just features/price.

**Current Plan's Competitive Narrative:**

*Section 5 (Competitive Advantages):*
1. Unified organizer + shopper platform ✅
2. Transparent, low pricing ✅
3. AI-powered tagging ✅
4. Mobile-first PWA ✅
5. Real-time auctions ✅
6. Customer support ✅
7. Modern design ✅
8. Network effects ✅

**Assessment:** This list is excellent strategically but reads like a feature checklist. It doesn't tell the *story* of why competitors can't catch up.

**What's Missing — The Moat Narrative:**

The plan doesn't articulate **workflow stickiness** as the core defensibility mechanism:
- Organizers who list once reach ALL shopper types (treasure hunters, resellers, casual browsers, decorators)
- Shoppers who find one sale discover an ecosystem of organizers across all sale types
- Unified search/discovery creates switching costs that single-category competitors can't match
- Network effects aren't just "more organizers → more inventory"; they're "organizers earn more per sale because they reach a consolidated shopper base"

**Current narrative:** "FindA.Sale is the only platform combining X, Y, Z"
**Stronger narrative:** "Organizers choose FindA.Sale once and reach every type of buyer. Shoppers choose once and find every type of sale. That's a moat competitors can't replicate."

**Fix Needed:**

Rewrite Section 5 (Competitive Advantages) to lead with moat narrative:

> **Competitive Advantage: Unified Ecosystem Creates Workflow Stickiness**
>
> Every competitor specializes. EstateSales.NET focuses on estate sales. AuctionNinja on auctions. DIY Auctions on budgets. No competitor spans all community resale types — and that's their vulnerability.
>
> When an organizer lists on FindA.Sale, they reach treasure hunters, resellers, casual shoppers, decorators, and collectors in one place. When a shopper finds their first treasure on FindA.Sale, they discover an entire ecosystem of events. Once organizers list here, they earn more. Once shoppers find treasures here, they stay. That's workflow stickiness — the kind competitors can't buy or copy.
>
> Everything else (AI, mobile, support) is table stakes. But unified discovery across all sale types? That's the moat.
